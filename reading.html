List of books I have enjoyed reading and I would recommend to others.
  • "The Ph.D. Grind: A Ph.D. Student Memoir" by Philip J. Guo (read) — I got a better idea of what challenges to expect as a PhD student and as a professional working in academia.
  • "Designing Data-Intensive Applications" by Martin Kleppmann
  • "Code Complete: A Practical Handbook of Software Construction (2nd Edition)" by Steve McConnell
  • "Building Microservices" by Sam Newman — I read this with a good friend of mine. We would read for 30 minutes and discuss what we had just learned over lunch.
  • "The Software Craftsman: Professionalism, Pragmatism, Pride" by Sandro Mancuso — Professionalism for software engineers.
  • "Extreme Programming Explained: Embrace Change" by Robert C. Martin
  • "Writing for Computer Science" by Justin Zobel (website) — I learned about the process of research, from reading to write-up, the advisor-student relationship, managing research like an agile (as opposed to waterfall) project to produce results, and much more.
  • "The Information: A History, A Theory, A Flood" by James Gleick — Science and history are two of my favorite topics. This book includes both. It is about the history of science. I learned about the evolution of ideas and inventions such as the telegraph and the Difference Machine, etc.
  • "Communicating Sequential Processes" by Tony Hoare (read) — Hoare defines a beautiful concise mathematical language for describing complex concurrent processes.
  • "Art & Fear: Observations On the Perils (and Rewards) of Artmaking" by David Bayles, Ted Orland
  • "These Truths: A History of the United States" by Jill Lepore (NYT review) — Beautiful prose, beautiful history.
  • "The Five Dysfunctions of a Team: A Leadership Fable" by Patrick Lencioni
  • "The Craft of Research" by Wayne C. Booth — I learned about the importance of writing and how important it is to start writing on day one.
  • "Freedom Evolves" by Daniel C. Dennett
  • "The Wisdom of Teams: Creating the High-Performance Organization" by Douglas Smith and Jon Katzenbach — This book taught me that in companies teams are a better performance measure than individuals. And that a team is more than a few people who sit next to each other and work on the same project.
  • "The Concept of Law" by H. L. A. Hart — I enjoyed the prose and the challenge of formulating a unifying concept and the analysis of the previous attempts was entertaining to read.
  • "Fooled by Randomness" by Nassim Taleb — A lot of behavioral economics experiments and stories. On a side note, I believe everything outside your control and knowledge appears random to you.
  • "The Black Swan" by Nassim Taleb
  • "The Blind Watchmaker" by Richard Dawkins — One of the first books that made me interested in evolution and biologically inspired algorithms.
  • "Six Thinking Hats" by Edward de Bono
  • "Team Geek" by Collins-Sussman and Brian Fitzpatrick
  • "Architecting for Scale" by Lee Atchison
  • "Site Reliability Engineering" by Niall Richard Murphy et al. (read)
  • "Clean Architecture" by Robert C. Martin
  • "The English Language: A Very Short Introduction" by Simon Horobin — Where did English come from? And how has it evolved into the language used today?
  • "A Tour of C++" by Bjarne Stroustrup (website)
  • "The Go Programming Language" by D&K
  • "The Design of Everyday Things" by Don Norman
  • "The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy" by Douglas Adams — One of the funniest books I have read.
  • "Isaac Newton" by James Gleick — The evolution of his ideas, the history of scientific publication and the Royal Society.
  • "The Healthy Programmer: Get Fit, Feel Better, and Keep Coding" by Joe Kutner
  • "Leading" by Alex Ferguson
  • "The Grasshopper: Games, Life and Utopia" by Bernard Suits — Philosophizing about games.
  • "The Problems of Philosophy" by Bertrand Russell — Russell is one of my favorite thinkers.
  • "Thinking Fast & Slow" by Daniel Kahneman
  • "The Go Programming Language Phrasebook" by David Chisnall
  • "Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much" by Eldar Shafir and Sendhil Mullainathan — Subjective scarcity influences decision making.
  • "Explore It! Reduce Risk and Increase Confidence with Exploratory Testing" by Elisabeth Hendrickson
  • "Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind" by Harari
  • "Genetic Programming" by John Koza
  • "Finding Flow: The Psychology Of Engagement With Everyday Life" by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi
  • "Theory of Fun for Game Design" by Raph Koster. Ratey
  • "This is Marketing" by Seth Godin — The crux of marketing explained.
  • "Think: A Compelling Introduction to Philosophy" by Simon Blackburn — Very readable introduction to important philosophical ideas explained in their historical context.
  • "The Sense of Style: The Thinking Person's Guide to Writing in the 21st Century" by Steven Pinker
  • "The Structure of Scientific Revolutions" by Thomas Kuhn — How scientific progress is made.
  • "The Course of Love" by Alain de Botton — A novel about love and marriage as it could happen in the real world.
  • "Adolphe" by Benjamin Constant — French novel.
  • "Conquest of Happiness" by Bertrand Russell — I love the Russell's optimism and his analytical reasoning.
  • "Aura" by Carlos Fuentes — Unique writing with second-person voice.
  • "Range: Why Generalists Triumph in a Specialized World" by David Epstein
  • "Creativity, Inc." by Ed Catmull — Lessons learned about running Pixar by its founder.
  • "Go in Action" by Erik Saint Martin et al.
  • "The Schopenhauer Cure" by Irvin Yalom
  • "When Nietzsche Wept" by Irvin Yalom — A novel about Nietzsche, Breuer, Freud and psychoanalysis.
  • "The Remains of the Day" by Kazuo Ishiguro — I felt like Mr. Stevens when I was organizing a hackathon, specially during the meals while I was making sure we had enough food and everyone was happy and had everything they needed to focus on their project :)
  • "Go Design Patterns" by Mario Castro Contreras
  • "When Breath Becomes Air" by Paul Kalanithi — Inspiring yet sad story of a hard working doctor.
  • "Without Feathers" by Woody Allan
